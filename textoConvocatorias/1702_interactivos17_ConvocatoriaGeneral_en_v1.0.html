Medialab-Prado is launching an open call for the submission of projects and research articles for the international collaborative prototyping seminar and workshop to be held in Madrid from 17 to 31 May 2017. As a theme, Interactivos?’17 suggests mobility in the city (living beings, material goods, pollutants, etc) on the intersection of three wide perspectives: citizen science, environmental sustainability and creativity and art with digital tools.

The mentors for the workshop are Daniel Lombraña, Miguel Álvarez, Christian Nold and Karolina Sobecka. Anthony Townsend and Fabien Girardin are also participating among others. The rest of the names will be added as soon as they are confirmed.

Introduction

Medialab-Prado is launching an open call for the submission of projects and research articles within Interactivos? framework. This edition of Interactivos? welcomes the submission of projects, research articles or theoretical works which showcase, explore, imagine, prototype and experiment with and for movement in the city and its multiple forms and possibilities: not only the more visible movement of pedestrians, vehicles or goods but also that which is less obvious, that of suspended particulate matter, hidden fauna and waste generated by urban activity.

In your approach to this theme we suggest exploring the intersections between three standpoints: citizen science, environmental sustainability and creativity and art with digital tools. This is a broad and flexible thematic framework which takes a critical stance to explore various aspects such as the decline in public spaces for community living and gathering, the unequal access to these spaces and public services, the capability of the various modes of transport to meet mobility needs, construction of today's culture of mobility, the impact on climate change, environmental deterioration, the decline in local biodiversity, remodelling of the landscape, safety, etc.


Approach to the workshop by the mentors and speakers

Miguel Álvarez Martínez

"The ballet of the good city sidewalk never repeats itself from place to place, and in any one place is always replete with new improvisations." Jane Jacobs.

The lorry that delivers the soft drinks that will be served at the outdoor café, which obstructs the path of the bicycle that wants to park beside the ambulance coming to collect the grandmother to take her to a consultation in the same hospital where they took the boy that left school running after a ball and fell down between the cars which thank goodness were stuck in the traffic jam caused by the striking taxi drivers dissatisfied with the competition they face from that app that lets you get from A to B without having to worry about anything else during the journey, giving you time to think about what mobility is, or rather, if there’s anything that is not mobility.


Christian Nold

Cities are both material things and ideas but this combination does not make them vague. Instead they are full of many kinds of specificities and controversies that are spread across the city.

For example urban pollution exists in many different places, as particles in our lungs, mental health problems, databases, political movements and tedious technical and policy reports.

How do you deal with this complexity? Is it simply a matter of building our own sensor box or can we find new ways to intervene and design in response to these issues? My suggestion is that controversies are great opportunities for playful and critical experimentation.

One approach I can offer is ontological design that can map controversies in terms of the different realities at stake and build prototypes that can introduce new actors and realities.


Anthony Townsend

After a century of development, automated vehicles (AVs) are finally rolling into cities. From Pittsburgh to Helsinki, Sao Paulo to Singapore, over the next twenty years cities will see the fastest change in technology transportation in history.

At first, AVs will change the experience of travel. Without the need to drive, we can turn our attention to work, recreation and social communication. But as citizens and businesses explore new uses of this technology, it will also change how and why we travel. We’ll find new ways to share trips, new reasons to go on them in the first place, and start and end trips in different locations and at different times than we do now.

Over time, these changes will add up to big shifts in the shape of the city. Some activities may spread out into the far reaches of the countryside. Others may pile up in new an interesting ways. The harmony of commerce, recreation, and civic life will change as profoundly as the shift from classical to jazz. The morphing choreography of city life will drive changes in land use, buildings and public spaces, and the livelihoods and economies that make use of them.

But it’s impossible for us to know how this will turn out. The future city, and the mobility solutions that shape it, are ours to design.



What do the workshops consist of? 

Interactivos? is a research and production platform for the creative and educational uses of technology. Its main goal is to expand on the use of electronic and software tools for artists, designers and educators, thus contributing to the development of local communities of cultural producers in this field.

Interactivos? events are a hybrid between an international production workshop, a seminar and a showcase. After the first days of seminar, projects promoters and collaborators work together to develop the proposed prototypes. Then these prototypes are showcased in a public exhibition in Medialab-Prado. Thus a space for reflection, research, and collaborative work is created. The process is open to the public from beginning to end.

In more detail:

From the proposals people submit, a maximum of 6 articles and 8 projects will be selected. Collaborators can register until few days before the workshop starts.

The workshop will be held from 17 to 31 May. Over the first two days there will be a seminar open to the public in which international experts and the authors of the selected articles will present their work. This will be a space in which to inspire and be inspired, to get to know others and to make yourself known. On these days the promoters of the selected projects will tell us what they aim to do in the workshop and everyone present will have a chance to meet each other.

Following the 2-day seminar the production workshop itself will begin, wherein promoters will develop the proposed prototypes, working hand in hand with collaborators. This workshop will be intensive and exciting: a space for exchange and experimentation. We will be helped along by coaches who will provide assistance and advice throughout the process. 

The workshop will end with a public presentation of the resulting prototypes. The prototypes will then be included in an exhibition at Medialab-Prado

Core themes:

In this regard we specify the following three standpoints from which to observe movement in the city:
  1. Environmental sustainability [ref], construed as a complex web of symbiotic relationships between very different mechanisms and organisms, taking an ecosystemic approach wherein caring for humankind may well mean caring for the ecosystem that sustains us.
  2. Citizen science, as a set of methodologies that enable citizens to participate and to make science. Citizen Science offers practices and examples of how to ask the right questions and how to develop methodologies that help mitigate or even solve some of the problems that parasitise our environments [ref, ref]. 
  3. Creative use of digital tools, which along with critical reflection and art can change the way we observe and experience our environment, in order to search for new doors and utopias to explore. 
These three standpoints are proposed in a broad, open and flexible format. If well-justified, moving away from one of them will not be a deciding factor in the open call selection process.
 

A Bit of Background

The city is a complex system [ref], and the interrelationships between its components are such that every problem must be observed from various perspectives in order to propose overarching solutions. Herein lies a paradox, in that activities that initially seem as obvious as being able to enter or exit a city like Madrid on foot are actually not so simple [ref]. The mobility of people in the city is a delicate and controversial topic, as it affects many spheres of everyday life. Decisions to introduce measures like reducing road traffic to cut pollution thus end up being strongly criticised. Figures such as those supplied by the WHO, indicating that pollution alone causes the death of 7,000 people in Spain per year [ref, ref], reveal the urgent need to come up with alternatives. The fact that residents of the Community of Madrid spend an average of 78 minutes per day travelling to and from work is disturbing. To put this into context, if we consider that the year has 247 working days, every year the average Madrid resident wastes (or invests?) more than 13 days commuting, with an annual associated cost of over €3,000 [ref].

People, however, are not the only things that move. Waste also travels: every day more than 400 lorries carrying over 4,000 kilos of refuse arrive at the Valdemingomez rubbish dump. Is this a lot or a little? According to this  article we would need just seven and a half years to build a mountain as tall as the Teide out of this amount of waste.

This scenario of pollution, traffic jams and rubbish originating solely from human beings affects innocent victims: plants and animals that suffer because of our actions. Is this important? To know that 63% of Europe's sparrows have vanished over 30 years [ref], or that we're currently in the midst of the sixth mass extinction [ref, ref] may be a matter indefference for someone. Since we eat chicken, not sparrow, what does it matter if we lose some species, i.e. biodiversity? The mental frameworks forged by specialising in a subject at university or in our careers may narrow our perspective: just like the human body and the city, nature is an interrelated complex system whose every component plays an essential role. From bacteria to whales, biodiversity is crucial for the balance of any ecosystem. The city is in fact an ecosystem, albeit one unknown to many people [ref]. A clear example of these interrelationships is the direct connection that exists between pollution, the disappearance of bees, the increased price of some foods in the supermarket, financial losses among producers and the fall in tax revenues (not to mention crops lost due to frosts, hail, etc., which increase with climate change) [ref, ref].

As Ricoeur said, a city with no utopia is a city with no purpose. Moreover, given that we are moving towards a global dystopia it may be time to re-imagine this purpose and explore new utopias: to search for new forms of doing and thinking, to experiment with new citizen spaces where multiple disciplines converge and work together to create a living, inhabited, complex and balanced city. But where do we start? Perhaps…Are Smart Cities, with the associated energy consumption entailed, the solution to all our ills [ref]? Are Luc Schuiten's 'vegetal cities' the answer [ref]? Do we need to plan cities on a human scale [ref] or is the problem that there aren't enough roundabouts [ref]? Could the solution involve creating enormous urban food farms to contribute towards sustainability, or might these further unbalance the urban ecosystem [ref, ref]? Is citizen science a key ally in order to mitigate the impact of unsustainable forms of mobility [ref, ref]? Or could biotechnology perhaps be the new paradigm that will enable us to grow and pollute for a bit longer yet [ref, ref]? Perhaps we need to avoid urban spaces where you can meet and converse, forcing all public spaces to be spaces for passing through, for transit, turning them into [non-places]?
